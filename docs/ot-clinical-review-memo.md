# OT Clinical Review Memo

## Purpose

This memo is the working document for clinical and scope-of-practice review by EFI's occupational therapy advisor.

The goal is not to turn EFI into a clinical treatment site. The goal is to:

- improve clinical precision
- tighten referral boundaries
- reduce overstatement risk
- improve special-populations language
- strengthen applied usefulness for practitioners, educators, and families

This review should focus on whether EFI's public claims, tools, curriculum language, and coaching guidance are accurate, safe, and appropriately bounded.

## Review standards

Please review each item for:

1. Clinical accuracy
2. Scope-of-practice safety
3. Referral clarity
4. Population sensitivity
5. Practical usefulness
6. Overclaim / underqualification risk

For each issue, use one of these tags:

- `critical` — should be corrected before public reliance
- `important` — should be corrected in the next revision wave
- `nice-to-have` — improves precision or usefulness but is not urgent

For each note, please include:

- page or file
- quoted phrase or section heading
- concern
- suggested replacement or revision
- reason for change

## Questions to answer during review

### Clinical precision

- Are any pages implying diagnosis, treatment, or therapeutic effect where EFI should be describing coaching, education, or skills support instead?
- Are ADHD, ASD, trauma, anxiety, mood, sensory, or school-functioning references phrased with enough caution and accuracy?
- Are there places where clinical language is too absolute or too simplified?

### Scope and referrals

- Are coaching vs therapy boundaries clear enough?
- Are referral triggers specific enough?
- Are there places where stronger “refer out” language is needed?
- Are there any implied promises that could be read as clinical care, advocacy, or protected professional practice?

### Applied usefulness

- Which pages would benefit most from OT-informed examples, environmental supports, sensory-context notes, or school/home implementation details?
- Are the intervention examples realistic across child, adolescent, and adult populations?
- Are the tools likely to be interpreted correctly by families or professionals without overreaching?

### Special populations and risk flags

- Are there missing cautions for:
  - sensory overload
  - autistic learners
  - trauma-affected learners
  - anxiety-related shutdown / avoidance
  - school refusal
  - caregiver burden
- Are there places where the site should distinguish skill deficit from performance inconsistency more carefully?

## OT-specific prompts

Use this section to capture expertise EFI should incorporate more explicitly.

### Environmental supports

- Where should EFI emphasize environmental engineering more strongly?
- Where should physical setup, sensory load, transitions, object placement, and routine scaffolds be more concrete?

### Functional performance

- Where should EFI distinguish “knows what to do” from “can perform consistently in context” more clearly?
- Where should the site say more about fatigue, regulation, initiation, recovery time, or task conditions?

### Family and school translation

- Where would OT-informed examples improve the home-school bridge?
- Where should caregiver coaching, teacher communication, or classroom participation language be tightened?

## Public-facing use of review

If EFI references this review publicly, the language should be exact. Use only what has been explicitly approved.

Preferred examples:

- `Reviewed for scope-of-practice clarity by an occupational therapy advisor.`
- `Clinically reviewed for referral-boundary language and special-populations phrasing.`

Avoid implying:

- accreditation
- medical endorsement
- therapy supervision
- clinical treatment authority
