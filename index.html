Field Notes

What the research actually says

Russell A. Barkley, PhD

“ADHD is not a disorder of knowing what to do; it is a disorder of doing what you know.”

Executive function is performance at the point of action — the gap between intention and execution — not a deficit of information or willpower.

Taking Charge of ADHD — Russell A. Barkley

Thomas E. Brown, PhD

“ADHD is essentially a name for developmental impairment of executive functions.”

Attention is not one thing; it is a coordinated system of activation, focus, effort, emotion, memory, and action working in concert.

Smart but Stuck — Thomas E. Brown

Peg Dawson & Richard Guare

“Executive skills are the cognitive processes required to plan and direct activities, including task initiation and follow-through.”

Good design meets the brain where it is. When the environment does the remembering, the child or adult can finally do the thinking.

Smart but Scattered — Dawson & Guare

Sarah Ward, MS, CCC-SLP

“Executive function is the ability to see the future — to picture yourself doing a task and work backward from that image to now.”

Strategy begins with mental imagery. If you cannot picture the finish, you cannot plan the start.

Cognitive Connections — Sarah Ward

John J. Ratey, MD

“Exercise is the single most powerful tool we have to optimize the function of our brains.”

Movement upregulates dopamine, norepinephrine, and BDNF — the exact chemistry that supports focus, regulation, and follow-through.

Spark: The Revolutionary New Science of Exercise and the Brain

Edward M. Hallowell, MD

“ADHD is not a disability; it is a way of being in the world. It can be a gift when understood and managed well.”

The same wiring that produces distractibility also produces curiosity, creativity, and intensity. The work is channeling the current, not shutting it off.

Driven to Distraction — Hallowell & Ratey

Adele Diamond, PhD

“Executive functions are more important for school readiness than IQ. They predict both academic and life success across the lifespan.”

Core EF skills — inhibition, working memory, and cognitive flexibility — are trainable. Practice, challenge, and joy are the ingredients.

Executive Functions — Annual Review of Psychology, 2013

Center on the Developing Child, Harvard

“Children aren’t born with executive function skills — but they are born with the potential to develop them.”

Scaffolding, repetition, and supportive relationships build the neural architecture for planning, self-control, and working memory.

Harvard Center on the Developing Child

J. Russell Ramsay, PhD

“The issue for adults with ADHD is rarely knowing what to do. It is implementing what they already know, consistently, over time.”

Cognitive-behavioral coaching closes the implementation gap by making the invisible friction visible and workable.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Adult ADHD — Ramsay & Rostain

Daniel J. Siegel, MD

“Where attention goes, neural firing flows, and neural connection grows.”

Self-regulation is a relational skill first. Co-regulation with a safe other is how emotional and executive circuits get wired in the first place.

The Developing Mind — Daniel J. Siegel

How coaching works

Three columns, one outcome: systems that hold under real life

Most coaching that gets bought here is a 12–16 week engagement. The work runs in three predictable phases.

1. Intake & pattern read

We map your executive functioning across five areas, audit your environment, and name the friction explicitly. You leave the first sessions with language for what's been happening.

2. System design

We build small, specific scaffolds at the point of performance — the desk, the calendar, the morning, the inbox. Designed for how you actually move, not how you wish you moved.

3. Iteration that sticks

Most systems collapse the second week. We run weekly sessions, debug what didn't work, and tune the design until it survives a real Tuesday at 4pm.
