What Families Usually Bring to the First Call

The issue is rarely raw intelligence. It is usually a translation gap between a student's creative strengths and reliable daily execution at home, in class, and under stress.

"They're so smart, but..."

Your child aces tests when engaged but "forgets" to turn in homework. Their potential is obvious—so why isn't it showing up?

Tutoring feels like punishment

You've tried tutors, but sitting through more "school after school" creates resistance, not results.

The IEP maze is overwhelming

"Preferential seating" and "extended time" sound helpful—but you're not sure your child is getting what they actually need.

Executive Functioning is the missing piece

It's not about being smarter. It's about planning, prioritizing, and following through—skills that can be trained.

What EFI is working toward Better initiation, less conflict, clearer systems, and more realistic support around the way a student actually functions. Outcomes vary by student, context, and consistency of practice.

Observed Session Logic

Intervention surfaces are chosen for the executive function demand, not for novelty.

EFI treats improv, chess, and cubing as structured rehearsal environments. Each format makes a different invisible skill observable enough to coach.

Core Intervention Surface

The "Yes, And..." Framework

Improv is where EFI trains flexibility live. Students have to respond, recover, listen, and keep moving without freezing when the plan changes.

Primary UseActivation + cognitive flexibility
Best ForRigid thinking, shutdowns, social hesitation
  • Builds fast recovery after mistakes
  • Turns active listening into a visible skill
  • Creates low-stakes repetition for adaptive thinking

How this can show up in practice: Some students who used improv reset prompts began rejoining tasks after mistakes rather than abandoning the block. Individual results vary.

The "Checkmate" Protocol

Chess externalizes pause-before-action thinking, making impulse control and consequence tracking visible in real time.

Best for: planning, inhibition, consequence awareness

How this can show up in practice: Some students have transferred the pause-before-act habit from chess into academic tasks, applying the same scan-choose-check sequence before submitting work. Individual results vary.

Chess Trains Pause-Before-Action →

The "Algorithm" Approach

Speed cubing shows students that hard tasks can be decomposed into procedures, checkpoints, and recovery moves.

Best for: working memory, frustration tolerance, procedural follow-through

How this can show up in practice: Some students have transferred the algorithm concept into academic workflow—breaking multi-step assignments into checklists and progressing through them in sequence. Individual results vary.

Cubing Makes Procedures Visible →

Session Surfaces

What the coaching work should feel like in real life

  • During sessionsStudents rehearse pause-before-action, recovery, and sequence thinking through concrete activities rather than generic motivational talk.
  • At homeFamilies leave with visible scripts, handoff routines, and lower-conflict planning language they can actually reuse during the week.
  • At schoolThe same routines get translated into assignment launch, missing-work recovery, and better communication with teachers.

Who This Works Best For

EFI is especially effective for creative students who think divergently, improvise naturally, and struggle with rigid systems that never adapt to how their brain actually works.

Creative but inconsistent

Big ideas, strong curiosity, weak follow-through once novelty drops.

Fast thinker, hard starter

Can solve hard problems verbally but stalls on initiating assignments and routines.

Emotionally intense learner

Frustration spikes quickly; needs visible recovery scripts and low-shame repetition.

Not a fit (or not yet) when...

  • The family wants pure tutoring with no executive-function behavior change work.
  • There is no willingness to practice routines between sessions.
  • The student currently needs a higher clinical level of care than coaching can provide.
